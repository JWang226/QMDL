# Koashi-Imoto Structure Theorem

**Appears in:** [[Article]] (Sec. 5, Prop. 4), [[Letter]] (converse discussion)

## Intuition

Suppose you have a set of quantum states and you want to compress them without disturbing any state in the set. The Koashi-Imoto theorem tells you exactly how much you can throw away: it decomposes the Hilbert space into a direct sum of tensor products $\bigoplus_j \mathcal{H}_j^L \otimes \mathcal{H}_j^R$, where the states vary only over the "left" factors $\mathcal{H}_j^L$ and leave the "right" factors $\mathcal{H}_j^R$ completely untouched. The right factors are redundant noise that can be discarded, while the left factors are the irreducible core that must be fully stored.

The punchline for this project: if the set of states is rich enough to generate the full matrix algebra on the Hilbert space, then there are *no* redundant factors at all. Every degree of freedom matters, and no compression is possible beyond storing the entire space.

## The Argument in the Converse (Article, Proposition 4)

The key application is **Proposition 4** (Irreducible orbit sector compression). Within a single Schur sector $\mathcal{H}_\lambda$, the encoder sees the orbit ensemble $\{\rho_{g,\lambda} = U_\lambda(g) \rho_\lambda U_\lambda(g)^\dagger : g \in \mathrm{U}(d)\}$ but does not know which $g$ was prepared (the code is "$g$-blind"). The proof proceeds in three steps:

### Step 1: The highest-weight projector lies in the generated algebra

Let $|v_\lambda\rangle$ be the highest-weight vector of the irrep $\mathcal{H}_\lambda$. Because $\rho$ has non-degenerate spectrum, the eigenvalue of $\rho_\lambda$ on the highest-weight state is $x^\lambda / s_\lambda(x)$, which is strictly larger than every other eigenvalue. Since this top eigenspace is one-dimensional, the spectral projector $|v_\lambda\rangle\langle v_\lambda|$ can be written as a polynomial in $\rho_\lambda$ via the Lagrange interpolation formula:

$$|v_\lambda\rangle\langle v_\lambda| = \prod_{j \neq 1} \frac{\rho_\lambda - x_j \mathbb{I}}{x_1 - x_j}$$

This is built from scalar multiplication, the identity, and powers of $\rho_\lambda$, so it lies in the $C^*$-algebra $\mathfrak{A}_\lambda$ generated by the orbit.

### Step 2: The $\mathrm{U}(d)$ orbit of the projector spans all rank-1 projectors

Conjugating by the group action gives $U_\lambda(g)|v_\lambda\rangle\langle v_\lambda|U_\lambda(g)^\dagger \in \mathfrak{A}_\lambda$ for every $g$. Now suppose $X$ commutes with every element of $\mathfrak{A}_\lambda$. Then $X$ commutes with every rank-1 projector $|u_g\rangle\langle u_g|$ where $|u_g\rangle = U_\lambda(g)|v_\lambda\rangle$. This forces each $|u_g\rangle$ to be an eigenvector of $X$, with eigenvalue $a(g)$. Since $a(g)$ is a continuous function on the connected group $\mathrm{U}(d)$ taking values in the finite set $\mathrm{spec}(X)$, it must be constant. So $X = a \cdot \mathbb{I}$.

### Step 3: The bicommutant theorem gives the full matrix algebra

The orbit $\{U_\lambda(g)|v_\lambda\rangle\}$ spans all of $\mathcal{H}_\lambda$ (because it is a nonzero invariant subspace of the irrep, hence the whole space). So $X = a \cdot \mathbb{I}$ on all of $\mathcal{H}_\lambda$, proving $\mathfrak{A}_\lambda' = \mathbb{C} \cdot \mathbb{I}$. By the finite-dimensional bicommutant theorem:

$$\mathfrak{A}_\lambda = \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H}_\lambda)$$

### Applying Koashi-Imoto

Since the generated algebra is the *full* matrix algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H}_\lambda)$, the Koashi-Imoto decomposition is completely trivial: there are no redundant right factors and no classical block structure to exploit. The entire irrep space is the "core" that must be stored. Therefore, any $g$-blind code for this sector with vanishing Haar-average error must satisfy:

$$|M_n| \geq \log \dim \mathcal{H}_\lambda + o(1)$$

This is the tight lower bound that makes the converse match the achievability.

## Why This Matters

The Koashi-Imoto theorem converts an algebraic fact (the orbit generates the full matrix algebra) into an information-theoretic conclusion (you cannot compress below $\log \dim \mathcal{H}_\lambda$). Without this theorem, you would know that the states "spread out" over $\mathcal{H}_\lambda$ but would not have a rigorous way to rule out clever compression tricks that exploit correlations between the states. Koashi-Imoto says: if the algebra is $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$, then there are no tricks -- you must store every qubit.
